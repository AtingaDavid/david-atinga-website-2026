About

Applied Economist &
Development Researcher

I am an applied economist with a PhD from the University of Tokyo (MEXT Government Scholar, 2021–2024), Department of Agricultural and Resource Economics, and over a decade of experience in applied research, impact evaluation, monitoring and evaluation, and international development consulting across Sub-Saharan Africa.

My research sits at the intersection of agricultural economics, climate variability, and household welfare. I study how weather shocks and climate change shape agricultural technology adoption and disadoption decisions, input investment, food security outcomes, energy choices, and the structural transformation of rural economies — with a primary focus on smallholder farmers in West and East Africa.

I grew up in Nyankpala, Northern Ghana — a farming and research community — which gives me a grounded, firsthand understanding of the realities facing smallholder households that informs all my research and consulting work. I have designed and supervised large-scale household surveys across 108 villages, led field research teams of 30+, and worked with JICA, USAID, IITA, UKAID, DFID, FAO, IFPRI, World Bank, IFAD-connected, and WFP-related partners, producing peer-reviewed research published in Q1/Q2 international journals.

I am currently based in Coon Rapids, MN, USA, actively seeking academic positions, postdoctoral fellowships, research roles, and international development consultancy opportunities in agricultural economics, development economics, food security, and monitoring & evaluation.

Research

Publications &
Working Papers

My research programme explores how climate variability and weather shocks shape the agricultural decisions, livelihoods, and food security of smallholder farm households in Sub-Saharan Africa — using rigorous microeconometrics, panel data, field-based evidence, and increasingly, satellite-derived climate and geospatial data integrated with household survey data.

My empirical work applies diverse methods including difference-in-differences, instrumental variables, propensity score matching, panel fixed effects, and structural models — translating findings into actionable policy recommendations for governments and development partners.

Current projects extend this work to examine weather variability and structural transformation in agriculture and rural labour markets, conflict exposure, and the long-term spillover effects of Green Revolution investments in Sub-Saharan Africa.

01
2024 · Frontiers in Environmental Economics
Analyzing the effect of severe weather on farmers' fertilizer usage and input investment amidst decreasing productivity in single-season agroecosystems
Atinga, D. (2024)
Frontiers in Environmental Economics (Agricultural Economics Section), 3, 1360513
doi.org/10.3389/frevc.2024.1360513 ↗
This article evaluates the enduring impact of fluctuating weather patterns in a single-cropping agroecological region on rice yields, examining the consequent influence on the utilisation of inorganic fertilizers and agricultural input investment among rain-fed farm households. The study investigates disparities, reasons, and underlying mechanisms driving changes in farmer input decisions under weather stress — with critical implications for climate-smart agriculture policy in West Africa.

02
2021 · International Journal of Educational Development
To attend or not to attend: Examining the relationship between food hardship, school attendance and education expenditure
Martey, E., Etwire, P.M. & Atinga, D. (2021)
International Journal of Educational Development, 80, 102304
doi.org/10.1016/j.ijedudev.2020.102304 ↗
This study examines the relationship between food hardship, school attendance, and education expenditure using nationally representative data from a survey of 14,009 households in Ghana. Results reveal significant adverse pathways from household food insecurity to children's educational outcomes and investment — demonstrating the cross-sectoral spillover costs of food insecurity for human capital development.

03
2021 · Energy
Household Energy Choice for Cooking among the Time and Consumption Poor in Ghana
Martey, E., Etwire, P.M., Atinga, D. & Yevu, M. (2021)
Energy, 226, 120408
doi.org/10.1016/j.energy.2021.120408 ↗
SDG Seven recognises access to modern energy for all. This study examines how time poverty and consumption poverty jointly determine the choice of cooking fuel among Ghanaian households. Time poverty reduces adoption of solid biomass fuel (SBF) while consumption poverty increases it. Promotion of labour-saving technologies is necessary to reduce household drudgery and facilitate energy transitions among the poor — with clear implications for SDG 7 policy design.

04
2021 · Agricultural Systems
Climate change, soil water conservation, and productivity: Evidence from cocoa farmers in Ghana
Amfo, B., Ali, E. B. & Atinga, D. (2021)
Agricultural Systems, 191, 103172
doi.org/10.1016/j.agsy.2021.103172 ↗
Impacts of climate change on soil water make soil water conservation practices crucial in cocoa production. This study assessed the nexus between soil water conservation practices, climate change mitigation, and cocoa productivity in Ghana. A positive inter-causal synergy exists between soil water conservation and cocoa productivity under climate stress — with direct relevance for IFAD's and World Bank's sustainable agriculture investments in West Africa.

Teaching

Teaching &
Student Mentorship

As an applied economist, I see teaching as a transformative avenue rooted in knowledge sharing, clarity, practical engagement with continuous learning, and relearning. My teaching philosophy is shaped by a deep respect for inclusive and interdisciplinary learning, and the belief that economics becomes truly powerful when students can connect theory with lived experiences and applied research.

Teaching Philosophy

I strive to make academic learning interactive and research-driven. I design my teaching around four core principles: making economics understandable through relatable examples and real-world analogies; fostering active learning through discussions, debates, and hands-on problem-solving; promoting equity and inclusion by building accessible learning environments that respect diverse backgrounds; and connecting theory to lived realities by integrating current events, global issues, and applied evidence into every course.

I am prepared to teach core courses including Principles of Microeconomics, Applied Econometrics, Development Economics, Environmental and Resource Economics, Agricultural Economics, and Food Security Policy. I welcome opportunities to supervise senior theses, collaborate on research projects, and involve students — especially from first-generation or underrepresented backgrounds — in hands-on, data-driven research.
